# Tomato — Early Blight (Alternaria solani)

## Symptoms
Dark brown to black lesions with concentric rings ("target spot" pattern) on
older, lower leaves first. Yellowing of surrounding tissue. Can also affect
stems (dark, sunken cankers) and fruit (leathery lesions near the stem end).

## Conditions favoring disease
Warm temperatures (24–29°C) alternating with periods of high humidity or
leaf wetness. Stressed or nutrient-deficient plants are more susceptible.

## Fertilizer recommendation
- Ensure adequate but not excessive nitrogen; nutrient-stressed plants
  (especially low nitrogen or potassium) are markedly more susceptible.
- Balanced fertilization with attention to potassium supports plant
  resilience against fungal pathogens.
- Avoid overhead irrigation combined with heavy fertigation, which extends
  leaf wetness periods.

## Pesticide / fungicide recommendation
- Chlorothalonil or copper-based fungicides applied preventatively, starting
  when the first fruit clusters form.
- Azoxystrobin or difenoconazole for curative control once lesions appear.
- Rotate fungicide modes of action to reduce resistance risk.

## Cultural practices
- Remove and destroy infected lower leaves promptly.
- Stake or cage plants to improve airflow and reduce soil splash onto
  foliage.
- Mulch around the base of plants to reduce spore splash from soil.
- Rotate tomato plantings with non-solanaceous crops.

## Severity notes
Early blight rarely kills plants outright but can significantly reduce
yield and fruit quality if left unmanaged, especially later in the season.
