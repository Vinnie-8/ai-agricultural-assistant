# Tomato & Potato — Late Blight (Phytophthora infestans)

## Symptoms
Water-soaked, pale green to brown lesions on leaves that expand rapidly,
often with a white fungal growth visible on the underside in humid
conditions. Stems develop dark brown streaks. On potato, tubers show
reddish-brown granular rot. On tomato, fruit shows firm, brown, greasy-
looking lesions.

## Conditions favoring disease
Cool, wet weather (15–20°C) with high humidity (>90%) and extended leaf
wetness. This is one of the fastest-spreading and most destructive plant
diseases under favorable conditions — historically responsible for the
Irish Potato Famine.

## Fertilizer recommendation
- Avoid excess nitrogen, which produces lush, dense canopies that trap
  humidity and favor disease spread.
- Ensure adequate potassium, which is linked to improved late blight
  tolerance in both tomato and potato.

## Pesticide / fungicide recommendation
- This disease spreads extremely fast — preventative fungicide application
  is critical, not just reactive treatment.
- Copper-based fungicides or mancozeb for preventative control.
- Systemic fungicides such as metalaxyl or cymoxanil for curative action
  once infection is confirmed, combined with a protectant fungicide.
- Destroy severely infected plants promptly to reduce spread to
  neighboring fields.

## Cultural practices
- Use certified disease-free seed potatoes.
- Avoid overhead irrigation; use drip irrigation where possible.
- Increase plant spacing to improve airflow.
- Monitor weather forecasts — cool, wet conditions warrant proactive
  fungicide application even before symptoms appear.

## Severity notes
Late blight can destroy an entire field within days under favorable
conditions. This is considered a high-priority, fast-response disease.
